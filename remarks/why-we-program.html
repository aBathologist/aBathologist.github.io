Why We Program

<2016-10-31 Mon>

Educators, generals, dieticians, psychologists, and parents program. Armies, students, and some societies are programmed. An assault on large problems employs a succession of programs, most of which spring into existence en route. These programs are rife with issues that appear to be particular to the problem at hand. To appreciate programming as an intellectual activity in its own right you must turn to computer programming; you must read and write computer programs …

— SICP

A bit of history

My formal training was in the soft side of the humanities: philosophy, literary theory, sociology, pedagogy, philology. Driven by a love of logic, academic ineptitude, and the search for a systematic method of coherent inquiry & exposition, I developed a passion for programming and an intense interest in computer science, generally, but programming language theory (PLT) in particular. I found no one in my academic milieu who really shared these interests, but there was often common intellectual ground. During my years recovering from academia, I believed, naively, that if only I could meet people who programmed, I'd find companions who shared my particular interests.

I have arrived at a career in IT and the more I meet and converse with people shaping the soft wares that stock our information age, the more I am aware of the rich variety in the experience of code and the vast disparities between different approaches to programming. I've gathered some notes documenting these differences, and some attempts to articulate what programming means to me, both in terms of semantics and in terms of praxis.

For the purposes of this essay, let's say a rational is a principle of the gathering of elements into a thing.
